\section{Visualization Design Principles for Churn Analysis}

\subsection{Audience and Analytical Questions}
We designed the visualization artifact for analysts and customer-retention
stakeholders. The dashboard does not only report how many customers churned; it
also points to customer groups above the baseline churn rate and helps the team
decide which groups deserve retention attention.

The core analytical questions are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item What is the overall churn baseline?
  \item Which customer segments are large enough to matter operationally?
  \item Which segments have churn rates above the baseline?
  \item Which features are actionable for a retention team?
  \item How do model outputs support, but not replace, business interpretation?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Metric Semantics}
We separate distribution metrics from risk metrics throughout the report. This
distinction matters because a segment can contain many customers but still have
an average churn rate, while a smaller segment can have a much higher churn
rate.

\subsection{Chart Selection by Analytical Task}
We chose chart types by task rather than by visual style. The churn dataset is
mainly table data, so the dashboard relies on bar charts, distribution views,
relationship views, and summary tables.

\centering
\caption{Chart types used for churn analysis tasks}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{p{0.22\textwidth}p{0.30\textwidth}X}
\toprule
\textbf{Task} & \textbf{Recommended chart} & \textbf{Churn example} \\
\midrule

Compare risk & Bar chart with baseline & Churn rate by complaint group \\
Show distribution & Histogram or box plot & Credit score or monthly charges \\
Show composition & 100\% stacked bar & Share of churned customers by segment \\
Show relationship & Scatter, heatmap, or small multiples & Satisfaction vs. complaints \\

As a team, we chose horizontal bar charts for most category comparisons because
many labels are long, such as \textit{Number of Service Calls},
\textit{Customer Satisfaction}, and \textit{Month-to-month}. Horizontal bars
keep these labels readable while still using bar length for comparison.

\subsection{Visual Encoding Rules}
In the final dashboard, we rely on encodings that are easy to decode during a
short presentation:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Position and bar length compare counts, churn rates, and
  feature importance.
  \item Color hue separates categorical states such as churn versus
  non-churn or low/medium/high risk.
  \item Baseline lines on churn-rate charts make above-baseline groups easy to
  detect.
  \item We avoid pie charts, donut charts, and 3D charts where accurate
  comparison matters.
  \item Color is not the only carrier of meaning; we pair color with
  labels, ordering, or reference lines.
\end{itemize}

We keep the 9.92\% overall churn rate as the main reference point because it
prevents a common reading mistake: a large customer segment is not necessarily a
high-risk segment. The baseline lets viewers compare each group by risk rather
than by size alone.

\subsection{Color System}
The final dashboard uses a gray--orange--blue direction rather than a red-green
palette. This was a team decision after reviewing the dashboard screenshots:
orange is visible as a warning color, blue works as the main comparison color,
and gray keeps supporting context quiet. The palette is more readable for
color-vision-deficient users than red-green contrast, and we still support the
colors with labels, sorting, and titles so the interpretation does not depend on
hue alone.

These colors remain stable across pages. Orange is reserved for warning or
highest-risk emphasis; blue carries the main selected comparison; gray provides
context. When color highlights a high-risk segment, the chart also uses a label,
sorting, or baseline marker so that the message remains readable without color.

\subsection{Dynamic Risk-by-Feature Chart}
The most technically complex dashboard visual is the dynamic bar chart for
\textit{Average Predicted Churn Risk by Selected Feature}. In the Prediction
page, a single-select feature slicer controls which business feature is shown,
while the bar chart displays the categories inside that selected feature. We use
a long-format table so one chart can switch between contract type, late
payments, satisfaction, complaints, service calls, and the other selected
features.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Required long-format fields for the dynamic risk chart}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{p{0.38\textwidth}X}
\toprule
\textbf{Field} & \textbf{Role in the visual} \\
\midrule
\code{feature} & Technical feature name from the data pipeline. \\
\code{feature\_display} & Readable business name used in the slicer. \\
\code{category} & Category shown on the bar-chart axis after a feature is selected. \\
\code{customers} & Number of scored customers in the selected category. \\
\code{predicted\_churn} & Number of customers above the prediction threshold. \\
\code{avg\_churn\_probability\_pct} & Average predicted churn probability in percent. \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

The slicer uses \code{feature\_display}, not the technical \code{feature} field.
The chart uses \code{category} on the category axis and
\code{avg\_churn\_probability\_pct} on the value axis. The value aggregation is
\textbf{Average}, not Sum, because probabilities are not additive. This is why
the bars in this chart are not expected to add up to 100\%: each bar is a
category-level average predicted probability, not a share of customers.

We also use the wording \textit{Average Predicted Churn Risk} deliberately. It
reminds the presenter that the chart compares average probability by category,
not the distribution of customers across risk levels.

The chart is sorted in descending order so the highest-risk category appears
first. Tooltips provide customer count, predicted churn count, and average
predicted probability. The scope is also explicit: this dynamic risk chart is
calculated from active non-churn customers scored by the model, while the
separate high/medium/low risk summary on the model-evaluation page represents
the test-period evaluation.

\subsection{Page-Level Visualization Structure}
We read the final visualization artifact as a compact story rather than a
collection of independent charts:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Overview}: shows total customers, churn count, non-churn count,
  baseline churn rate, and risk-level distribution.
  \item \textbf{Churn drivers}: compares feature-group churn rates against the
  baseline and shows top model feature importance.
  \item \textbf{Churn-only composition}: shows the share of churned customers by
  selected segment, while clearly stating that the denominator is churned
  customers only.
  \item \textbf{Model evaluation}: shows AUC, accuracy, precision, recall,
  F1-score, confusion matrix, and a short interpretation of model limitations.
  \item \textbf{Prediction and retention priority}: shows predicted churn
  customers, risk levels, and the features that define priority groups.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Figure Quality Checklist}
We used the following checklist when reading the final screenshots:
\begin{itemize}
  \item The title states either the analytical question or the main insight.
  \item The denominator of every percentage or rate is explicit.
  \item Count views and churn-rate views are not mixed into one ambiguous
  metric.
  \item Churn-rate charts include the 9.92\% baseline reference.
  \item Category order is meaningful: ordinal groups keep natural order, while
  nominal groups are sorted by value only when ranking is intended.
  \item Colors are semantic and consistent across pages.
  \item The chart avoids 3D effects, unnecessary decoration, and hard-to-read
  legends.
\end{itemize}
